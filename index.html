What is trading?

Trading is the act of buying and selling financial assets — stocks, cryptocurrencies, forex pairs — with the goal of profiting from price movements.

Unlike long-term investing, trading focuses on shorter time horizons: from minutes to weeks. Traders use systematic tools called indicators to study past price behaviour and make higher-probability decisions.

The most important lesson: no indicator predicts the future. They all measure what has already happened. What they give you is an edge — a slightly better chance of being right — when used correctly and consistently.

This guide covers the full spectrum of technical analysis: from basic moving averages to advanced market structure concepts used by professional traders worldwide.

Price action

Every asset has a price driven by supply, demand, news and sentiment. Price always tells the truth.

Candlestick charts

The standard way to visualise price. Each candle shows open, high, low and close for any time period.

Volume

How many units were traded. High volume validates a price move; low volume makes it suspicious.

Technical indicators

Mathematical formulas applied to price data that generate signals for entries, exits and trend direction.

Risk management

Rules that protect your capital. More important than any indicator — it determines if you survive long enough to learn.

The core decision

Buy, Sell, or Hold?

Every moment in trading comes down to one of three decisions. Indicators replace emotion with data so you make them consistently.

Buy

Enter a long position

You believe the price is going up. You purchase the asset to sell later at a higher price for a profit.

  • Uptrend confirmed by indicators
  • Momentum is building
  • Not overbought (RSI < 70)
  • Volume supports the move
Sell

Exit or short position

Close a winning trade to lock in profit, cut a losing trade to limit damage, or bet on price falling.

  • Trend is reversing
  • Overbought signal (RSI > 70)
  • Stop-loss level hit
  • Profit target reached
Hold

Wait for clarity

No clear signal means no trade. Doing nothing is often the best decision. Patience is a genuine trading edge.

  • Conflicting indicator signals
  • Low volume / no momentum
  • Choppy sideways price action
  • Waiting for confirmation

Before you trade a single dollar

Risk management

A great strategy with poor risk management loses money. A mediocre strategy with great risk management survives long enough to improve.

01

The 1% rule

Never risk more than 1–2% of your account per trade. With $1,000, that's $10–$20 max loss. You survive 50 consecutive losses.

02

Always use a stop-loss

A pre-set exit price that limits your loss automatically. Set it before entering every trade — no exceptions, ever.

03

Risk/reward of at least 1:2

Potential profit must be at least double potential loss. You can be wrong 50% of the time and still come out ahead.

04

Paper trade first

Practice with fake money for at least 4 weeks before using real capital. Most exchanges offer free simulation modes.
